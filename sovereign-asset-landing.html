Confirmed Deceased Owners
"Proceeds Due Beneficiaries" is not a data field. It's a confirmed death signal. When an insurance company reports unclaimed proceeds owed to beneficiaries, the original policyholder is deceased. That property is in probate, in transition, or about to be. No other platform surfaces this signal at scale. We filter for it specifically and cross-reference the address against county assessor records to confirm real property ownership simultaneously.
◎
Entity Unmasking at Scale
Trusts and LLCs own property. County assessors return the entity name, not the human. Our pipeline cross-references Secretary of State registries, tax billing addresses, voter rolls, and utility records to extract the Ultimate Beneficial Owner — the actual human being making decisions. When a trust also appears in the unclaimed property database, you have confirmed mismanagement or a deceased trustee. That's the highest-quality distress signal that exists.
⊕
Dual Revenue Architecture
One lead. Two checks. The same deceased estate owner has both unclaimed financial assets (insurance proceeds, dividends, mineral rights) and real property in transition. A finder agreement on the financial asset generates 10% of the recovered amount. A wholesale contract on the real property generates a $10K–$50K assignment fee. No other data product positions you for both simultaneously from a single record.

Due Diligence

QUESTIONS SERIOUS
OPERATORS ASK

Who is the customer for this data? +
Primary: Real estate investors and wholesale operators targeting estate and probate properties before they hit the MLS. The unclaimed property layer identifies confirmed deceased owners — the highest-quality off-market distress signal available at scale.

Secondary: Estate attorneys, heir hunting firms, and wealth recovery specialists who need fresh, structured lead feeds rather than running their own database infrastructure.

Tertiary: Hedge funds and family offices building distressed asset acquisition pipelines who require programmatic data access rather than platform subscriptions.
Is this better than existing databases like PropStream or BatchLeads? +
It's not a replacement — it's a layer that doesn't exist in those platforms. PropStream, BatchLeads, and ATTOM all source from the same public property records. None of them cross-reference the unclaimed property database. That cross-reference is the product.

When a trust appears in both the county assessor database and the state unclaimed property registry with "Proceeds Due Beneficiaries" — that's a confirmed deceased trustee, an estate in transition, and a real property likely available off-market. No existing commercial platform surfaces that signal. We do.
Why does a large dataset matter if the dollar values are hidden? +
State treasury systems cap displayed values at "$100+" — no exact amounts are shown, by design. This is actually the moat.

Because value is hidden from everyone equally, the only way to extract signal from a masked dataset is volume combined with holder classification. We know which insurers and financial institutions historically pay out at which tiers. Liberty National Life insurance endowments historically range $500–$50K. American General annuity proceeds range $1K–$500K+. Principal Financial Group dividends on trust accounts range $5K–$500K+.

That holder classification table took time to build. Scale is the unlock. 50,000 tiered records beats 50 random records every time — even when nobody can see the exact dollar amount.
Will state governments change their websites and break access? +
Short term (1–2 years): Low risk. States are legally required to maintain public unclaimed property databases under NAUPA compliance. The data obligation is statutory — it cannot be removed, only the access method can change.

Medium term (2–5 years): Moderate risk. States may add stronger bot protection or move to authenticated portals. Alabama's Turnstile implementation on detail pages is an early signal of this trend.

Mitigation: We don't store static snapshots — we run a live engine. When access methods change, we update the scraper. The data obligation never disappears. First-mover advantage is now, before this access window closes.
Is scraping state treasury databases legal? +
State unclaimed property databases are public records, maintained and published specifically for the purpose of reuniting owners with their assets. Programmatic access to publicly available government data for commercial purposes has been consistently upheld — most recently affirmed in hiQ Labs v. LinkedIn (9th Circuit, 2022) establishing that scraping publicly accessible data does not violate the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act.

The finder service model is specifically regulated at the state level — most states permit finder agreements with fee caps (Alabama: 10%) — meaning the entire commercial model is explicitly anticipated and accommodated by the legal framework that governs this data.
What does "dual revenue" actually mean in practice? +
One confirmed estate lead can generate two separate revenue events from a single contact:

Revenue 1 — Finder Fee: The deceased owner has unclaimed financial assets (life insurance proceeds, annuity payments, bank accounts, mineral rights royalties) held by the state treasury. A finder agreement gives you 10% of the recovered amount in exchange for identifying the asset and assisting the heirs in filing the claim. This requires no capital and no real estate license.

Revenue 2 — Wholesale Assignment Fee: The same deceased owner almost certainly had real property. That property is now in an estate or probate situation — heirs are motivated to sell, often below market. A wholesale contract with a $10K–$50K assignment fee to a cash buyer is a standard transaction in this scenario.

Same lead. One outreach. Two checks. That's the architecture.
